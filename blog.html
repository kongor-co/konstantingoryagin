Funny or? /but? /and? Professional

What do you think about having fun in the workplace?

I often hear colleagues and friends describe their workplaces as “fun” and that’s usually mentioned as a reason they enjoy their jobs and feel engaged. On the flip side, when a workplace isn’t fun, it’s often a reason people start looking elsewhere.

That got me thinking: what do we actually mean by “fun” at work? And is it truly at odds with being professional?

The perception of humor

In many organizations, fun is viewed as frivolous, childish, or even unprofessional. I believe that the ability to bring lightness and humor into serious environments is not a sign of incompetence — it’s a sign of confidence, emotional intelligence, and strong professionalism.

When you hear the word “fun,” what comes to mind? Laughter, jokes, play, spontaneity, smiles.

Now think of “professional.” Competent, reliable, organized, knowledgeable… maybe even strict or serious.

It’s easy to see why these two ideas feel incompatible. But is that perception based on reality — or just some beliefs or fears?

Humor requires maturity and confidence

Using humor at work doesn’t mean being a clown. It means being emotionally intelligent enough to understand:

  • The emotional climate of the room
  • The timing and tone that fits the moment
  • The ability to read others and adjust accordingly

In short, it takes both humility and a healthy ego to use humor well — without turning it into a performance or a distraction. Not everyone is naturally good at this. And that’s okay. I think humor at work should be treated as a skill, not just a personality trait.

Humor is cultural

Humor doesn’t travel equally across borders, industries, or teams. A joke that lands perfectly in a Berlin startup might feel awkward, or even offensive, in a Tokyo headquarters or even among the colleagues from Japan working for that same Berlin startup. Humor is highly cultural, and perceptions of tone and appropriateness vary widely.

Working in an international or multicultural team, it always helps to pause before dropping that punchline and ask yourself:

  • Is this joke universally relatable?
  • Could it confuse or alienate someone from a different background?
  • Would observational or light self-deprecating humor be a safer choice?

Positive and negative humor

Not all humor is equally humorous.

  • Positive humor builds trust, encourages inclusion, and helps people manage stress.
  • Negative humor — like sarcasm, ridicule, or self-deprecating jokes used to mask discomfort — can erode psychological safety, damage confidence, and strain relationships.

A company influences humor culture too

Humor is often seen as an individual trait, but companies play a big role in shaping the culture around it. Here are some examples from my experience and what I have seen around, of how organizations can help create space for healthy humor:

  • Provide trainings on emotional intelligence and inclusive communication
  • Support no-blame culture on all levels
  • Foster psychological safety so people feel okay speaking up if a joke crosses a line
  • Promote more “lightweight” attitudes — through rituals, social spaces, or humor-friendly onboarding

When humor is supported intentionally, it becomes a strength — not a liability.

The pressure to be funny

While humor can be powerful, it’s not always the right tool. And not everyone is comfortable using it. Some pitfalls I have experienced and that felt off:

  • Forced humor. Feeling like you always need to “keep it light” can be exhausting
  • Avoidance. Using humor to dodge difficult conversations can block real connection
  • Misfires. Even good intentions can backfire. What matters is how you handle it — with humility and accountability

Humor should never be mandatory, performative, or a substitute for meaningful dialogue.

Unexpected benefit of AI for interpersonal communication

What if learning to talk to AI could help us talk better to each other?

The more I work with AI models, the more I realize something unexpected: Prompting AI isn't just a technical skill — it's a communication masterclass.

When you write a good AI prompt, you learn to:

  • Be clear about your expectations
  • Provide context and background
  • Anticipate misunderstandings
  • Remove ambiguity
  • Refine based on feedback

Now imagine applying these same techniques to everyday human conversations.

Think about how often miscommunication happens because we assume the other person knows what we mean — when in reality, we're not being specific enough.

By practicing how to guide an AI to the outcome we want, we can also learn how to better guide conversations with colleagues, clients, friends — anyone.

The bonus? It creates space for empathy. When you focus on clarity and context, you're not just trying to be understood — you're also trying to understand.

So maybe learning to prompt AI well is really just training for what we all want more of: deeper human connection.
